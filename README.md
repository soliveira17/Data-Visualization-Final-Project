Data Cleaning Steps:

The original data files came in the form of .json files. Our first step was converting these to Tableau-readable CSV files. Since these files were very large, we found free online JSON to CSV file converters unable to handle the processing for these files. As such, we prompted ChatGPT to provide a detailed step-by-step description of how to use Python to convert the files. We followed ChatGPT’s instructions - downloading Python through Anaconda, launching Jupyter Notebook, preparing the .json files in a separate folder, and running the suggested code. While the code itself was successful in its first iteration, we continued prompting ChatGPT to adjust the code to better customize the resulting CSV sheets to output what we needed before connecting to Tableau. Below were our considerations: 
The business.json file is one of the main files we relied on for our Tableau dashboards, in which two of the columns were particularly useful for drawing insights from:
“attributes” - at first, the code ChatGPT suggested created an overarching “attributes” column; an example cell value looked like the following: “{"RestaurantsGoodForGroups":"False","RestaurantsDelivery":"False","OutdoorSeating":"False","RestaurantsReservations":"False","RestaurantsTakeOut":"True","GoodForKids":"False","WiFi":"'no'","BikeParking":"True","HasTV":"True"}”. However, since we wanted to use the individual attributes within the cell as part of our analysis, we prompted ChatGPT to provide additional code that would designate each individual attribute to its own column with its own corresponding values, i.e. column name = “RestaurantsGoodForGroups” with values “False”, “True” or “Null”.
“categories” - this category was a bit tricky in that multiple and often overlapping descriptors were used for each business; an example cell value looked like the following: “Mexican, Restaurants” or “Restaurants, Seafood, Nightlife, Japanese, Thai, Bars, Asian Fusion”. For the former, it was easy to categorize as “Mexican” cuisine. For the latter, the question was “Should we categorize this as Seafood, Japanese, Thai, Asian Fusion, etc?”. We took the approach of asking ChatGPT to provide us with Python code to output a list of categories sorted from most common to least common words - this was done by performing a count of the distinct keywords in the “categories” column. We asked ChatGPT to clean this list by removing any keywords that weren’t cuisines, such as “Seafood” or “Sandwiches”. With this clean cuisine-only list, we prompted ChatGPT to provide us with code to use the sorted list as an input for a new script and output a new CSV that replaces the original “categories” column with just 1 category, dictated by the keyword that is the lesser common word in the sorted list (see limitations that we ran into in detail below). As the Yelp Open Dataset includes many businesses beyond restaurants, any businesses that did not contain one of the cuisine keywords in our list was omitted completely. 

Project Limitations & Assumptions:

As mentioned above, the “categories” column had several descriptors per restaurant. The ones with only 1 or 2 descriptors such as “Mexican, Restaurants” or even “Chinese, Restaurants, Seafood, Noodles” were easier to definitively re-categorize as “Mexican” and “Chinese”, respectively. However, given that many restaurants had overlapping descriptors that would’ve fit into several of our designated categories (for example, “Chinese, Restaurants, Asian Fusion, Japanese, Sushi Bars, Thai”), we opted to design our code so that restaurants would be labelled with more niche cuisines first, based on the order of the least to most common keyword list that our Python code outputted. The limitation here is that the categorization is not an exact science, and the exact count of, say, “Asian Fusion” restaurants is reduced since niche/lesser common categories like “Vietnamese” or “Thai” would override it first. However, our rationale is that we wanted to be inclusive of the more specific/niche cuisines in our analysis instead of having an excess of broader categories like “Asian Fusion” lumped together. Another limitation is the ranking of the least to most common keyword list itself - for example, in our list, “Vietnamese” is ranked lower than “Thai”, so if a restaurant’s categories included both cuisines, “Thai” would be designated to that restaurant instead - this also reduces the true count of certain lesser common cuisines. 
Additionally, since categorization is manual given the available resources, we focused on cuisines only in our analysis. Given more time, it could be interesting to do a more detailed trend analysis that further breaks out cuisines into popular categories of foods such as ramen, boba, cocktail bar, frozen yogurt, etc.
Another limitation is the data set itself - we used the freely available Yelp Open Dataset that was put together for educational use. Our original proposal aimed to identify the most recent food & cuisine trends across the 3 major cities of NYC, Chicago & LA. However, these dataset files are not updated to the present day and are also not concentrated in major cities. In our search for a more up-to-date data set with similar attributes, we were unfortunately unable to find ones that were free and as comprehensive as the Yelp Open Dataset. As such, we decided to move forward with Yelp’s freely available data and therefore, the insights drawn & presented in our Tableau dashboards are not necessarily ones that can be readily used to create realistic action items from (if one were to do so). However, the overall objective and intent is the same and given the resources (money to purchase current datasets, time to extract and perform targeted cuisine categorization, etc.), we believe that our dashboard could be a useful asset for our intended audience.

Thought Process

We went into this project with an exploratory mindset, in which we were curious about which insights we could draw from the given dataset. We did not have any pre-planned agenda for a certain story to push to our intended audience yet. 
Upon further review of the data files, we opted to narrow down to the following 4 files given that the user.csv focused primarily around reviewer behavior at the individual user-level when we were more interested in trend insights at the business-level: 
business.csv
review.csv
checkin.csv
tip.csv
Once all data was clean, we connected these 4 files to Tableau and related the tables at the logical level, so as to keep the tables separate and retain the original respective levels of detail. For each relationship line, we set the Business Id field from the business.csv equal to the Business Id field from each of the other 3 files. 
Once set up in Tableau, we first played around to experiment different chart creations to see which graphs would reveal interesting patterns or characteristics about the cuisines. After creating a multitude of charts, we decided to focus on the following charts that we felt distinctly called out unexpected insights for the 3 cuisines of American (Traditional), American (New), and Cajun / Creole. In putting together the dashboard, we first prompted ChatGPT to help us come up with several high-level wireframes so we could visualize different set-ups and choose which ones felt most aligned visually. 

Dashboard Charts: 
KPI Tiles
Open Restaurants - Total number of restaurants included in the analysis. This KPI provides an overview of the dataset size and overall restaurant market coverage.
Total Reviews - Combined number of Yelp reviews across all selected cuisines and restaurants. This metric reflects overall consumer engagement and popularity within the analyzed food categories.
Total Check-Ins - Total number of customer check-ins recorded on Yelp for the selected restaurants. Check-ins act as a proxy for foot traffic, in-person dining activity, and real-world consumer interaction.
Average Star Rating - Average Yelp rating across all restaurants included in the dashboard. This KPI helps measure overall customer satisfaction and perceived dining quality.
Total Cuisines - Number of cuisine categories currently represented in the analysis. This provides context on the breadth and diversity of food types included in the dashboard view.
States Represented - Total number of geographic regions represented within the dataset. This KPI highlights the geographic reach and regional diversity of the restaurant trends being analyzed.
Bar Chart - Displays cuisines by the number of reviews, filtered to the top 10. One of our assumptions was that a higher number of reviews indicated more popularity for that cuisine, i.e. more people going & reviewing on Yelp. This chart highlighted that both American cuisines (new & traditional) were leading the Yelp review volume. 
Trend Chart - Shows the average reviews per restaurant by cuisine over time. For this chart, we first created a calculated field that divided the number of reviews by the count of distinct restaurants. Then, we separated this by cuisine and decided on a line chart as it was visually interesting to see the climbing trend for the cuisines, along with the expected steep drop during the pandemic. This chart brought out Cajun/Creole as the cuisine that showed the strongest review momentum per restaurant. 
Radar Chart - Shows the different attribute profiles of each of the 3 cuisines that stood out in previous graphs. We selected 7 attributes from the data set that we felt were ones people prioritized the most when looking for a restaurant - Delivery, Good For Groups, Good For Kids, Happy Hour, Has TV, Outdoor Seating, and Takeout. 
Quadrant Chart - Displays the top 10 cuisines (with Cajun/Creole and American New & Traditional specifically called out) in a quadrant format. We were curious about the relationship between whether a popular restaurant (higher review count) automatically meant a higher rating (more stars on Yelp). We found that Cajun/Creole was actually an underrated gem in which there were lesser reviews, but higher average star rating, compared to American (Traditional) which had significantly more reviews but a lesser average rating. However, it is also important to consider the volume of restaurants as an explanatory variable, indicated by the size of the cuisine bubble. For example, American (Traditional) has 4k+ distinct restaurants so it makes sense that more people = more opinions = more variation in dining experiences, ultimately affecting the average rating.  

Audience & Question

To support the needs of restaurant operators, investors, marketers, and consumers, our dashboard is designed to answer several strategic questions related to emerging cuisine trends, geographic differences in dining behavior, and the relationship between digital engagement and restaurant popularity.
Restaurant owners represent one of the primary audiences for this project because understanding emerging cuisine trends can directly influence business strategy and profitability. By identifying cuisines that are gaining momentum in big cities, restaurant owners can make more informed decisions about menu development, restaurant concepts, expansion opportunities, and marketing campaigns. For example, if Yelp review activity and restaurant openings suggest rising consumer interest in a specific cuisine, restaurant operators may choose to incorporate related menu items, launch pop-up concepts, or adjust their branding to align with shifting preferences. The ability to identify trends early can also help restaurants remain competitive in an industry that is highly influenced by consumer tastes and social media visibility.
Investors and entrepreneurs within the food and hospitality industry are another important audience for this analysis. Venture capital firms, restaurant groups, franchise operators, and independent investors often rely on market signals to determine where future growth opportunities may exist. Our project provides a data-driven perspective on which cuisines are experiencing increased engagement and expansion, potentially signaling underserved markets or high-growth opportunities. Investors could use these insights to evaluate restaurant concepts, identify promising geographic markets, or better understand evolving consumer demand patterns. In an industry where timing and trend adoption are critical, predictive insights derived from Yelp activity may help reduce uncertainty when making investment decisions.
Marketers and brand strategists may also benefit significantly from this project. Food trends increasingly spread through digital platforms, influencer culture, and online reviews, making consumer-generated content a valuable source of market intelligence. By analyzing Yelp reviews, ratings, and restaurant growth patterns, marketers can better understand how consumer sentiment evolves across different cuisines and cities. These insights can inform advertising campaigns, partnership strategies, influencer collaborations, and social media positioning. Additionally, marketers for food delivery apps, hospitality brands, and consumer packaged goods companies may use this type of analysis to anticipate future demand and tailor campaigns toward cuisines that are gaining popularity with younger, digitally engaged audiences.
Finally, this project may also appeal to MBA students and other graduate-level students across the United States who frequently explore dining experiences as part of their social and professional lives. Students in major urban areas often rely on recommendations, online reviews, and trending restaurants when choosing where to dine with classmates, friends, family, or networking contacts. By identifying cuisines and restaurant categories that are gaining popularity early, our analysis can help students discover emerging dining trends before they become mainstream. Beyond practical dining recommendations, the project also demonstrates how data visualization and consumer analytics can be applied to real-world lifestyle decisions, making it particularly relevant for students interested in strategy, marketing, entrepreneurship, hospitality, and consumer behavior.
